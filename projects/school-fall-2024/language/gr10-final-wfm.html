The Wee Free Men

Book report by gr10 Lee

Throughout The Wee Free Men, Terry Pratchett wrote about stereotypes and the harm they can cause, as well as advice on the most fulfilling way to live your life. He wrote about what the women that lived in the Chalk (a place named for its chalky turf) were expected to do, such as learning to write and getting married. He also wrote about how the children of the Chalk were expected to behave. 
But the point he especially focused on was about the harm that believing in stories without evidence can do. The main character, Tiffany, never believed the stories about the wicked old witch and the beautiful princess. Tiffany was intelligent and clever, unlike most children in the Chalk, and she didn't want to grow up just to get married like everyone else expected of her. She wanted to change the minds of the people in the Chalk and show them their errors. 

Early in the book, there was a story about an old woman called Mrs. Snapperly who was burned out of her home because she looked like a witch and had a black cat. Tiffany had never agreed with the actions the people in the Chalk took because of the false stories they'd believed, and she wanted to be a witch so she could prove those stories wrong. There was a passage towards the beginning of the book where Pratchett explained the reasons for Tiffany's disbelief in the storybooks and tales:

"And all the stories had, somewhere, the witch. The wicked old witch.
And Tiffany had thought, Where's the evidence?
The stories never said why she was wicked." --Page 27, The Wee Free Men, by Terry Pratchett.

By making Tiffany yearn for more evidence to back up the information in the stories, Pratchett explained to the readers why Tiffany[1] was skeptical about the stereotypes surrounding witches and fairies.

Pratchett proved the stereotypes wrong near the end of the book when he introduced a character called the Queen. The Queen was a beautiful elf, but she stole children and used dreams and nightmares to trap them in her world forever. She stole Tiffany's brother, Wentworth, which led to Tiffany crossing into the Queen's world and utilizing the dreamscape to get him back. During the rescue, she found the baron's son in the Queen's world, but he believed the stories about witches and tried to attack Tiffany. He thought the Queen was good because she was beautiful, and he was confused about Tiffany being a witch because she wasn't old and ugly like in the tales. However, by the end of the book, she managed to convince him that the stories he'd heard were wrong and not everything was as it was told.

There are several key takeaways from the Wee Free Men, but I think the most important one of them all is don't fall victim to stereotypes. Throughout the book, we meet characters who never questioned what they were told and never thought about it for themselves, and because of that, they did terrible things. Pratchett told us to use our minds to find the flaws in the logic and look for the answers ourselves. 
The second takeaway that arose near the end of the book was don't do good things for a reward; do them because they're right. Tiffany rescued the baron's son and her own brother, but because the baron's son was a boy and he was older than Tiffany, everyone thought it was him[2] who did the rescuing, and they wouldn't believe otherwise even when he tried to explain what had really[3] happened. Though it bugged Tiffany at first, in the end she was okay with it because she knew she had done the right thing and she didn't need recognition to know that. This is very important to remember. If it's not of the utmost importance, just let it go. 
The final important subject he talked about is[4] you have to do the work. You can't change the way things are going just by wishing for them to change; you have to work if you want to get anywhere in life. Sitting by and passively wishing that things were different just means that the people who are working to actually change things—whether you agree with it or not—are going to get what they want because they did the work. This was explained clearly in the beginning of the book:

"If you trust in yourself. . .and believe in your dreams. . .and follow your star. . . you'll still get beaten by people who spent their time working hard and learning things and weren't so lazy." --Said by Miss Tick, page 39, The Wee Free Men, by Terry Pratchett.

Overall, I really loved this book. It has an excellent plot, and Pratchett wrote in an engaging and funny way that still managed to get the main points across. The language and old Scottish words he used to describe the characters, speech, and landscapes were very interesting, and every character in the book was unique; even all the Wee Free Men (also called Feegles) who had the same word patterns and general appearance were easily distinguishable from each other. I absolutely recommend this book and would read it again.
